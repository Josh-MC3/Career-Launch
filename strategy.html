Job Search Strategy

Most people job-search backwards.

They blast applications into the void and wait — the least effective channel there is. Here's the strategy that actually moves people from searching to hired: see your funnel, then go around the front door.

Where the competition thinks the search ends

The hidden job market.

The jobs you can see are the jobs everyone can see — the most crowded, lowest-converting channel there is.

The visible-application route is where most candidates pile up. Referrals and internal connections sidestep that volume problem entirely: employee referrals can bypass strict filters and put a human advocate inside the process before you ever formally apply. Three levers do the work:

  • ✓
    Network into roles before they're posted. Many positions are filled through people the team already trusts.
  • ✓
    Get referred. A referral is the single strongest signal you can attach to an application.
  • ✓
    Build recruiter relationships. Internal and agency recruiters fill the same roles repeatedly — being known to them compounds.

The "how" is below — and it's not what most people fear it is.

The highest-leverage, most-avoided activity

Networking, without the ick.

The fix is treating it as information-gathering, not begging. People say yes to a 15-minute conversation far more than to "can you get me a job." Here are copy-ready messages — make each your own.

Cold outreach — someone in your target field
Hi [Name] — I'm exploring a move into [field] and your path at [company] stood out. Would you be open to a 15-minute call in the next couple of weeks? I'd love to hear how you got into it. Completely understand if you're swamped.
Alumni connection
Hi [Name] — fellow [school] grad here. I'm working toward [role/field] and saw you're at [company]. Could I ask you a few questions about your experience there? Even 15 minutes would mean a lot.
Reconnecting with a dormant contact
Hi [Name] — it's been a while! I've been following [their work/company] and I'm starting to look at opportunities in [field]. Would you have time for a quick catch-up call? Would be great to hear what you're up to.
The referral ask — only after a real conversation
Thanks again for the conversation last week — it really helped me think this through. I noticed [company] posted a [role] that lines up well with what we discussed. Would you feel comfortable referring me, or pointing me to the right person? No pressure either way.
Recruiter outreach
Hi [Name] — I'm a [your title] with [X years] in [domain], focused on [specialty]. I'd welcome the chance to be considered for relevant [role-type] openings. I've attached my resume and I'm happy to share more.

The cardinal rule: follow up, and give before you ask. The best networkers make deposits long before any withdrawal.

High reward, high competition

Remote roles.

The competition for remote roles is brutal precisely because everyone wants them — which makes targeting and referrals even more decisive than usual. What works: niche remote boards over the giant aggregators, a profile that signals you can work independently, and a resume that surfaces self-management and async-communication evidence (see the remote persona in the Resume Lab). Strong industries include tech, marketing, customer success, design, and parts of ops and finance.

Its own rules entirely

Federal jobs (USAJobs).

Federal hiring runs on rules where most private-sector advice actively hurts you. The federal resume is long and detailed — the opposite of the one-page norm — because it must document hours per week, salary, and detailed duties to establish that you meet rigid, stated qualifications. Keywords from the announcement matter enormously, and genuinely so: the screening is against the listed requirements. This is one of the few places "match the posting precisely" is literally true. Read each announcement's qualifications section like a checklist and mirror it honestly.

The federal resume's length is a feature, not a mistake — see the length guide in the Resume Lab.

From service to civilian career

The Veteran Transition Hub.

You have leadership, logistics, and grace-under-pressure experience most candidates can't touch. The only real problem is translation. Let's fix that.

🎖️ SkillBridge

The DoD program that lets transitioning service members do civilian industry training or an internship during their final months of service — while still drawing military pay.

It's one of the strongest on-ramps available: real civilian experience before you're even out. The honest catch — it's competitive and requires command approval, so start the conversation early.

🪖 Veteran preference

In federal hiring, veteran preference is a real, codified advantage in how applications are evaluated — points added to qualifying scores, with specific eligibility rules.

Clear-eyed framing: it's a meaningful edge in federal hiring specifically, not a private-sector guarantee. Pair it with the USAJobs guidance above.

🔁 Translate your experience

The trap is a resume full of rank and acronyms civilians can't parse. Lead with scope — people, budget, outcomes — in language any hiring manager understands.

✕ Untranslated "E-7 responsible for a platoon's logistics and readiness across two deployments."
✓ Translated "Operations leader managing 40 personnel and $2M in equipment across two overseas deployments, with full accountability for readiness, training, and supply."

More side-by-side examples live in the veteran persona of the Resume Lab, and the interview framing is in the Question Bank.

🔐 Cleared jobs

If you're leaving with an active security clearance, that clearance is a significant, marketable asset — employers pay a premium to avoid the cost and wait of sponsoring one.

Understand your clearance's status and reciprocity, and use dedicated boards like ClearanceJobs and Hiring Heroes that cater to this market.

🧭 The honest bar

Your experience is genuinely valuable — that's not in question. The work isn't proving it; it's translating it into words a civilian recruiter recognizes in the first ten seconds.
